# Epistemic Foundation 
_Conditions of Possibility_

###  Structure of an Argument
An argument is an organized set of propositions with premises offering reasons for adopting a belief. The structure of an argument can involve definitions of terms and scope, shared background and common ground, and stated or implicit assumptions. Premises connect to a conclusion through deductive, inductive, abductive, or practical reasoning. The conclusion matches the available support, whether certain, probable, or unknowable. In a well-formed argument, premises, assumptions, and inferential moves appear in forms allowing the audience to judge validity, strength, relevance, and adequacy.

### Mind is the Basis of Knowledge
Empirical knowledge of the world grows out of stable experience with objects and events. Stability rests on sensations ordered in time and space and linked by quantitative and causal rules. The mind brings and imposes structure onto experience, and empirical inquiry works within the same structure to uncover particular laws. Learning depends on knowledge prior to experience.[^1]

### The Action Axiom
Human action is purposeful conduct toward definite ends in situations with options and preferences. Such action involves choice under scarcity, acceptance of tradeoffs over time, and judgment used to speculate about an uncertain future. Features of human action flow from the nature of action and structure observation of behavior as meaningful conduct. From the same foundation, theory deduces implications of action and shapes the framework for empirical inquiry in the human sciences.[^2]

### The Argument Axiom
Humans argue. In argument, arguers take an action of seeking justification by offering reasons for a belief and inviting additional arguers to share the same belief. Argument ranks claims, treats some claims as true and other claims as false, and frames the contrast as a matter of right and wrong. Any one denying a claim offers a further argument and, in that move, practically relies on shared meaning, a method of inquiry, and an ethic in which reasons lead an arguer to a different state of mind. Through deliberate control of words and beliefs, arguers display responsible agency, answerable for judgment and bound by demands of coherence among stated beliefs. Features of argument make argument a route to knowledge and rest on the possibility of arguing, the standing of judgment, the role of reason, some form of self-ownership, and openness to learning.[^3]
