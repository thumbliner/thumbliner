# Epistemic Foundation 
_Conditions of Possibility_

###  Structure of an Argument
An argument is a set of propositions in which some of them, the premises, are offered as reasons to accept another, the conclusion. The structure of an argument includes definitions of key terms, clarification of scope, recognition of common ground, and explicit or implicit assumptions. Premises can support the conclusion through deductive, inductive, abductive, or practical forms of reasoning. Depending on the strength of that support, the conclusion may be treated as certain, probable, or unknowable. In a well-formed argument, these components and their inferential links are clear enough for an audience to judge their validity, strength, relevance, and adequacy.

### Mind is the Basis of Knowledge
Empirical knowledge grows from stable experience with objects and events. That stability rests on sensations ordered in time and space and linked by quantitative and causal rules. The mind lays structure over this experience, and empirical inquiry works within that same structure to uncover particular laws. Learning always leans on knowledge that comes before experience.[^1]

### The Action Axiom
Human action is purposeful conduct directed toward definite ends in situations with options and preferences. It involves choice under scarcity, accepting tradeoffs over time, and using judgment to speculate about an uncertain future. The features of human action follow from how we structure observation of behavior as meaningful conduct. From this foundation, theory deduces the implications of action and provides the framework for empirical inquiry in social sciences.[^2]

### The Argument Axiom
Humans argue. In argument, they take an action: offering reasons to justify a belief to an audience. Argument ranks claims, treating some as true and others as false, and casts the contrast as a matter of right and wrong. Any one who denies a claim offers a further argument and, in that move, practically relies on shared meaning, a method of inquiry, and an ethic in which reasons can lead an agent to a different state of mind. By deliberately choosing words and managing their beliefs, arguers show responsible agency: they are answerable for judgment and bound by the demands of coherence among what they say they believe.[^3] These features of argument make it a route to knowledge and rest on the possibility of arguing, the standing of judgment, the role of reason, self-ownership, and an openness to learning.
