# Epistemic Foundation 
_Conditions of Possibility_

###  Structure of an Argument
An argument is a set of propositions in which some (the premises) are offered as reasons to accept another (the conclusion). Its structure includes definitions of key terms, clarification of scope, recognition of common ground, and explicit or implicit assumptions. Premises support the conclusion through deductive, inductive, abductive, or practical forms of reasoning, and the resulting conclusion may be treated as certain, probable, or unknowable depending on the strength of that support. In a well-formed argument, these components and their inferential links are clear enough for an audience to judge validity, strength, relevance, and adequacy.

### Mind is the Basis of Knowledge
Empirical knowledge grows out of stable experience with objects and events. Stability rests on sensations ordered in time and space and linked by quantitative and causal rules. The mind imposes structure onto experience, and empirical inquiry works within the same structure to uncover particular laws. Learning depends on knowledge prior to experience.[^1]

### The Action Axiom
Human action is purposeful conduct toward definite ends in situations with options and preferences. Such action involves choice under scarcity, acceptance of tradeoffs over time, and judgment used to speculate about an uncertain future. Features of human action flow from the nature of action and structure observation of behavior as meaningful conduct. From the same foundation, theory deduces implications of action and shapes the framework for empirical inquiry in social sciences.[^2]

### The Argument Axiom
Humans argue. In argument, arguers take an action of offering reasons to justify a belief to an audience. Argument ranks claims, treats some claims as true and other claims as false, and frames the contrast as a matter of right and wrong. Any one denying a claim offers a further argument and, in that move, practically relies on shared meaning, a method of inquiry, and an ethic in which reasons lead an arguer to a different state of mind. Through deliberate control of words and beliefs, arguers display responsible agency, answerable for judgment and bound by demands of coherence among stated beliefs.[^3] Features of argument make argument a route to knowledge and rest on the possibility of arguing, the standing of judgment, the role of reason, self-ownership, and openness to learning.
